LLM Revision Diagnosis

Length assessment: 53 chapters is viable for a dual-protagonist "double novel," but the current generative draft reads over-distributed in the first half and middle. The back third has real structural mass; the opening and several mirrored mid-book runs look too granular for the amount of premise movement they deliver.
Structural health: Structurally, the novel is strongest from late Act Three into the Act Four climax. The mountain is real: Chapters 34-35 hit hard on the negative side, Chapter 37 acts as a hinge, Chapters 45-51 build with increasing positive alignment, and Chapter 51 is the unquestionable summit by energy, gravity, and premise force. Earlier sections show valleying, low prominence, and repeated local beats that do not always compound.
Premise alignment: Premise alignment is mixed but promising. The manuscript spends much of its mean magnitude slightly negative, which fits a long failure/descent phase, but too many chapters with decent theme sit near zero or weak alignment, suggesting uncertainty about what the story is "about" in motion. When the book commits—24, 25, 27, 30, 34, 35 on the descent side; 45, 48, 49, 51 on the ascent side—it becomes much sharper.
Distribution health: Energy and gravity are back-loaded. Act One has hook value but also the highest concentration of cut-risk and cheapness. Act Three contains both some of the book's best material and a drag pocket around the Earth/fantasy-tourism sequence. Act Four is the cleanest sustained run, though a few planning-and-bonding chapters repeat similar dual-protagonist functions. The Titus bonus chapter lands after emotional closure and currently behaves like detachable promo matter rather than integral epilogue.
Primary strength: The story's biggest strength is that its major trauma/reckoning chapters are not merely loud—they are premise-bearing. Katiya's arc in particular culminates in a structurally legitimate climax, and the dual-protagonist braid becomes most effective when both lines convert private damage into public action.
Primary risk: The biggest risk is mirrored-beat drag: the dual-novel gimmick sometimes produces paired scenes that echo each other without escalating the central premise. This is most visible in the early misunderstanding loop, the mid-book relationship/outing material, and some Act Four planning-grief-household chapters where emotional intent is clear but thematic vector is diluted.

Paragate has a real novel inside it already, and the metrics strongly support that the true book emerges in the late-middle through climax rather than in the current opening sprawl. The strongest spine is: descent through public/private failure, excavation of formative trauma, reversal into chosen truth and joint action, then Katiya's dawn confrontation. Revision should not flatten the double-novel concept, but it should get much harsher about asking whether each mirrored Cody/Katiya beat creates new vector or merely reflects prior material. Compress the early loop, tighten the Act Three tourism/romance drift, protect the trauma-and-reckoning core, and treat the Titus add-on as explicitly bonus material unless it can be made to pay rent for the main book.
