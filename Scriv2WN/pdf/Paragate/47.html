  Cody 

My keys slide out of the ignition, and after a long, sleepless night, I realize I'm sitting in Navidson-Monroe's dirt lot, seventeen minutes early to clocking in.

But I don't think I'll be clocking in today, because maybe they shouldn't know I'm here. I've been consistent for the last couple months: maybe I can just pass it off as a convenient accident for this day they're set to let me go, as I bide my time at my workstation, waiting for the perfect moment to put a suit on and rush to that distant conference room in eight hours.

I find a tremor in my hands when I reach beneath the seat to slide it back at a distance: about as much as an airline would give if you threw an extra hundred dollars at your ticket. The passenger-side window is curtained by the suit I last wore to present my senior project: kept pristine in a plastic bag from the cleaners, with a blue, paper tag stapled inside one of the button holes dated almost three years ago.

The space between the pedals and the seat is still poor enough to keep my legs from trembling as pitch the seat back, looking up to remember I have a sun roof for the first time in ages. Maybe I'd find sleep if I wasn't energized by a five-hour energy I guzzled to keep my eyes open: Katiya and I didn't let each other sleep, whether it was the pent-up passion on the long passage from haters to lovers, or the discomfort in the thought of sleep bringing us to the next day faster.

The thoughts about every way this mission can go wrong fill my mind for another fifteen minutes, until I can't take it any longer, and I step out in my father's clothes, removing the Pelican case from my trunk to store it under the car until Kei or Reed can inch it further to the aerospace tower on break.

But I need to get myself there, and yet all I ever want is to stay there in the safety of my car: finding that the feeling rhymes with that incessant drive to langush at the lowest state of energy; the feeling I find on nights where the emptiness is at its worst.

But the thing is that I told people I was doing this. I'll lose my home if I don't try — and for the first time in ages, it feels like I'm being pulled more by the luminous threads of a future dream than threatened by a scalding brand of failure.

And I think I actually want a result from something for the first time in forever, and I really want it, so much that I withdraw the old suit from the passenger side of the car and take my technician's bag in my other hand, barely able to contain a box of my brother's uniformed dress shoes that might still fit me.

I approach the door with a little piece of everyone upon me, slowing my roll then speeding up, then slow down again, as I normalize my speed with the stream of all the remaining technicians holding the door open for each other.

"You tryna take my fuckin' job, Dunsmore?" One of the technicians jokes, punching my other shoulder with his calloused hand. "Tryna get to floor manager so you can re-hire your dad?"

They chide each other in terms of crass deprecation, and it's why I've actually come to like my time on the floor.

"No, it's so I can fire ugly pukes like you," I say, earning a loud laugh from the other grisly technicians, distracting some suit-wearers from the Human Resources division.

"Your pops told me about your thing today," the same guy says. "You need someone to cover your work or stir a ruckus, let me know!"

"Or me!" Another says.

They clap me on the back as I sneak to my work station, hanging the suit from Dad's toolbench with a picture of my brother and I: the same wallpaper on my brother's old iPod that I lost. My father's advice echoes in my head, to keep those hands moving or else they'll come for you.

So I grab a wrench, cranking away at the drawings I once helped create with something other than despair, as I toil in the shadow of the reaper.
